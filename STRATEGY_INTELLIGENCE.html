Section 04B — Advanced Tactics

The Grey Zone

These are legal. All of them. Some are aggressive. All are used by companies larger than Halcyon every day. Know what you're doing and do it with intention.

Pay-to-Play Conference Sponsorships That Buy Editorial
Becker's, Modern Healthcare, HFMA, and RevCycleIntelligence all have sponsored content programs. A "contributed article" by Naitik Joshi on behavioral health billing appears in Becker's under your name — it is a sponsored placement but reads as editorial. $3K–$10K per placement. It generates inbound leads and PR credit. Do not confuse this with journalism — it's advertising with a byline. Effective.
Physician Group Referral Fee Structures
Healthcare Anti-Kickback Statute prohibits kickbacks for referrals of federal healthcare program patients. But paying a finder's fee to a practice manager, hospital consultant, or healthcare attorney who refers a private-pay or commercial-only practice is legal if structured correctly. Get healthcare counsel to draft a compliant referral agreement. Build a referral network of healthcare consultants, attorneys, and accountants who work with physician groups.
Advisory Board as a Sales Channel
A hospital CFO on your advisory board will refer peers — that's the real ROI, not the advice. Structure advisory agreements with specific deliverables: 2 introductions per quarter to their network. This is standard practice. Compensate fairly (0.1%–0.15% equity or $2K/month). The referral network of one hospital CFO is worth more than any marketing campaign.
LinkedIn Thought Leadership as Direct Sales
Revenue cycle directors, hospital CFOs, and practice administrators are on LinkedIn. A consistent publishing cadence (2× per week) on RCM-specific topics — denial rates, coding changes, payer policy updates — builds an audience of exact buyers. At 5,000 followers in this niche, inbound leads start coming. This is not social media marketing. It's a direct-response channel that compounds. No one in this space is doing it well.
Public Comment Submissions to CMS
CMS publishes proposed rules and requests for information. Submitting substantive public comments — under Halcyon's name — puts the company on record with CMS staff and gets the name into the federal register. CMS staff read these. Consultants and lobbyists who monitor CMS rulemaking see Halcyon's name. It's free, it builds regulatory credibility, and it signals that Halcyon is an operator, not just a vendor.
State Medicaid Advisory Committee Participation
Every state with Medicaid managed care runs advisory committees that are open to the public. Showing up consistently — as a billing company commenting on provider experience, claims processing timelines, and denial rates — gets Halcyon known by state HHS staff. State procurement decisions are made by people. Being a known, credible voice in a state's Medicaid advisory process is a direct path to vendor registration and eventual contract consideration.
The frame on all of this: None of these tactics are secrets. The PE-backed giants do all of them with bigger budgets. Your advantage is speed and authenticity — you can move faster, be more specific, and own a niche they can't focus on because their overhead requires $100M+ clients. Use that.

The GeBBS lesson: $850M at 17× EBITDA with only $50M EBITDA. You do not need to be enormous. GeBBS was acquired at a premium because it had: recurring revenue, offshore delivery efficiency, diversified US client base, and a clear AI/automation roadmap. The profile matters more than the size.

What PE Actually Looks For

Recurring, Predictable Revenue

Multi-year contracts or % of collections. PE firms underwrite cash flows — unpredictable revenue kills multiples. Your % of collections model is already structured correctly.

Low Customer Concentration

No single client >20% of revenue. PE firms fear customer concentration. Build a diversified book from the start — even if clients are smaller.

Defensible Niche

Specialty RCM (behavioral health, radiology, oncology) commands premium multiples. Francisco Partners backed Zotec specifically because specialty is harder to replicate. Own a niche before a larger firm does.

20–30%+ EBITDA Margins

Tech-enabled RCM companies hit 20–30%+. Pure services companies get 10–15%. The Halcyon model (small team + technology layer) should target 25%+ as it scales. This is what separates a 10× multiple from a 5× multiple.

The "Rule of 40"

Revenue growth rate + EBITDA margin ≥ 40%. Used as the standard benchmark for tech-enabled services. At 30% growth + 20% margin = 50. Hit Rule of 40 and you're in the conversation.

Finding 01
EQT Controls Both Layers

EQT owns Waystar (clearinghouse software, 29%) AND GeBBS (offshore BPO, $850M). They control the technology layer and the delivery layer of RCM simultaneously. No other PE firm has this dual position. This makes EQT the single most strategically important firm in the industry — and Waystar the most important channel partner for Halcyon to pursue.

Finding 02
Conifer Losing $143B Is Your Market

CommonSpirit is ending its $143B RCM outsourcing contract with Conifer to insource. When the largest outsourced hospital RCM relationships revert in-house, the market shifts toward modular, technology-enabled components. A Halcyon that owns AI-powered denial management or prior auth for a specialty is not threatened by this — it's what replaces Conifer-style full-service outsourcing.

Finding 03
The HBMA-OIG Accreditation Is Underused

HBMA's company-level compliance accreditation is co-developed with HHS's Office of Inspector General. Hospital procurement departments increasingly require compliance evidence before signing. This accreditation is the one credential that makes risk-averse hospital administrators say yes — and almost no small billing company has bothered to get it. It's yours to own.

Finding 04
Seema Verma Is the Network

Former CMS Administrator, now EVP at Oracle Health. Her network spans every hospital CFO, every state Medicaid director, and every senior CMS official in the country. She has been at TPG, Cressey & Co, Lumeris, Wellsky — she is comfortable advising startups. Getting an introduction to her network (not necessarily to her directly) is the single highest-value government-sector relationship move available.

Finding 05
GeBBS at 17× With Only $50M EBITDA

GeBBS was acquired at a premium multiple with a relatively modest EBITDA. The premium came from profile: recurring revenue, offshore delivery efficiency, AI/automation roadmap, diversified US client base. You don't need to be big to get a big multiple. You need the right profile. Build toward the profile from day one, not the size.

Finding 06
ICG's $1.2B Secondaries Bet Validates RCM

Intermediate Capital Group invested $1.2B as a secondaries co-investor in the TowerBrook/CD&R R1 RCM take-private. Secondaries funds don't make speculative bets — they underwrite cash flows. ICG's participation signals that RCM is now viewed as core healthcare infrastructure with predictable, durable cash flows. This is the strongest possible institutional validation of the sector.

Finding 07
TowerBrook Has Ascension Health Access

TowerBrook co-sponsors the ATHO fund (Ascension TowerBrook Healthcare Opportunities) with Ascension Health Alliance — one of the largest US Catholic health systems. This is a direct backdoor from TowerBrook PE into a massive hospital buyer of RCM services. If Halcyon can get into TowerBrook's orbit (through conferences, advisors, or eventual bolt-on), Ascension Health becomes a reachable enterprise client.

Finding 08
Veritas Capital Has Government Contractor DNA

Veritas is fundamentally a defense and government contractor PE firm. CEO Musallam's 10× Wash100 Award is a government contracting recognition. Their ownership of Athenahealth is a crossover play — but it means Athenahealth has access to VA, DoD, and federal health system contracts that a pure commercial PE firm wouldn't. The VA healthcare system is an underexplored RCM market.

Finding 09
KKR Is Betting on AI-Native RCM

KKR invested in Infinx Services (AI-driven prior auth, eligibility verification, claims — May 2024) while co-owning Cotiviti ($11B analytics platform). This is a clear signal: KKR's thesis is AI-native workflow automation alongside large-scale analytics. If Halcyon builds even a modest AI-assisted component (prior auth automation, eligibility checking) it positions as a KKR-thesis-aligned asset.

Finding 10
SBIR Reauthorization Timing Is Perfect

SBIR/STTR was reauthorized April 13, 2026 after a 5-month funding freeze. $4B+ in annual government innovation funding is back online. First post-reauthorization solicitations open now. An early application — before the backlog builds — has a better probability than an application filed 12 months from now after everyone else also noticed the reauthorization. Move fast.
